## Why Popular DIY Hacks Fail You

Most DIY scratch-removal tricks are well-meaning but flawed. Here’s why:

### **1. Food-Based Fixes (Walnuts, Mayonnaise, Coconut Oil)**  
These methods introduce oils and fats into the wood’s finish, causing:
- Sticky or oily residue  
- Dust and dirt accumulation  
- Rancid smells over time  
- Grain clogging that complicates future repairs  
They only darken the scratch — they do not repair the damage.

### **2. Vinegar + Olive Oil Mixtures**  
This combo is known to:
- Cloud or soften protective finishes  
- Leave an unpleasant odor  
- Attract dust  
Not ideal for long-term care.

### **3. Markers, Crayons, and Shoe Polish**  
These provide short-lived color camouflage but offer:
- No structural repair  
- Smear or transfer during cleaning  
- A patchy, mismatched appearance  
- Zero protection against deeper damage  

**Bottom line:** For a flawless, lasting repair, you need materials and techniques that match the scratch depth — not kitchen ingredients.

## The FurnishCare Pro Guide: Diagnosing Your Scratch

Accurate diagnosis is the heart of good repair work. Here’s how to identify exactly what you’re dealing with:

---

## **1. The Surface Scratch (Finish Only)**

**What it looks like:**  
- White lines, haze, or dull streaks  
- You cannot feel it with your fingernail  

### **DIY Approach**  
- Apply high-quality furniture polish or cream to fill micro-abrasions  
- Use a wax-based filler stick (matched to wood tone) for slightly deeper marks  

### **Pro Insight**  
Using the wrong product can create build-up that attracts dirt or dulls the finish.

---

## **2. The Medium Scratch (Shallow but in the Wood)**

**What it looks like:**  
- Light-colored groove  
- You *can* feel it with your fingernail  
- Wood fibers slightly exposed  

### **DIY Approach**  
- Touch-up markers or wood tint pens  
- A precise blend of iodine + denatured alcohol for darker woods (use carefully)  

### **Pro Insight**  
Color matching is an *art*. A wrong shade draws more attention than the scratch itself.  
Excess liquid can also raise the wood grain, leaving a textured patch.

---

## **3. The Deep Gouge (Wood Missing)**

**What it looks like:**  
- A deep groove  
- Wood is visibly removed  
- Tactile and highly noticeable  

### **DIY Approach**  
- Colored wood putty  
- Epoxy fillers  
- Melted shellac sticks followed by scraping and light sanding  
- Finish with stain matching and topcoat  

### **Pro Insight**  
This is a complex repair.  
Over-sanding or mismatched stain often results in an ugly blob-like patch — worse than the original gouge.  
Professional restoration is strongly recommended.
